## Navigating the Evolving Landscape: B2B Technology Buyer Behavior in the Global Energy Sector

## Executive Summary

The global energy sector is navigating an era of unprecedented transformation, driven by the dual imperatives of meeting escalating energy demand and accelerating the transition to a low -carbon future. This complex environment, characterized by economic volatility, geopolitical shifts, and stringent regulatory pressures, profoundly influences B2B technology procurement. Energy companies are increasingly investing in digital transformation, with Artificial Intelligence (AI), cloud computing, cybersecurity, a nd specialized solutions for decarbonization -such as Renewable Energy Management Systems (REMS), energy storage, and grid modernization -at the forefront of their priorities. However, the immense energy consumption of technologies like AI also presents new  challenges and opportunities for the sector.

B2B technology buying in the energy industry involves large, diverse committees, with decisions influenced by IT leadership, Line of Business heads, C -suite executives, and increasingly, ESG and Sustainability officers. The rise of self -directed buyer jour neys, where prospects conduct extensive independent research, necessitates a shift in marketing strategies. Buyers prioritize credible, data - backed content, including thought leadership, genuine case studies, product demos, and peer reviews, while the influence of traditional analyst reports is waning.

Messaging must resonate with core themes of value, ROI, sustainability, innovation, reliability, and security, tailored to specific buyer roles and journey stages. Regional variations are significant: North America focuses on grid modernization and domesti c clean energy incentives; EMEA sees growth in B2B energy services like e-mobility and hydrogen; and APAC is characterized by younger, digital - first buyers, larger buying committees, and a strong emphasis on localized GTM strategies and ESG considerations.

For technology marketers, success hinges on building trust through transparency and third - party validation, optimizing for discoverability across digital channels, adopting value-centric selling approaches, and effectively navigating complex buying committ ees with multi -threaded engagement. Leveraging data and analytics for GTM optimization and adapting strategies to distinct regional customs are critical. Ultimately, an "always -on" marketing approach, providing continuous value and being ready to engage informed buyers at any point in their non - line a r journe y, will be essential for navigating this dynamic market.

## 1. Industry Context: The Evolving Energy Landscape

The global energy sector is undergoing a period of profound and accelerated change. This transformation is not merely a cyclical adjustment but a fundamental reshaping of how energy is produced, distributed, and consumed. Understanding this evolving context is crucial for B2B technology providers aiming to effectively engage and serve energy industry clients. Key drivers include the global energy transition, pervasive economic and geopolitical influences, the rising importance of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) criteria, and distinct regional dynamics.

## The Global Energy Transition: Key Drivers and Imperatives

At the heart of the energy sector's evolution is the global energy transition, primarily driven by the urgent need for decarbonization and the collective ambition to achieve net-zero emissions by mid-century. 1  This transition is characterized by a significant and accelerating shift towards renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, and green hydrogen. Concurrently, there is a strategic move away from traditional fossil fuels, evidenced by the planned retirem ent of coal -fired power plants and a reduction in new upstream oil exploration activities. 1

The McKinsey Global Energy Perspective 2024 highlights that while considerable progress has been made since the Paris Agreement, the energy transition is now entering a "new phase, marked by rising costs, complexity, and increased technology challenges". 3 This suggests that the path forward will require even more sophisticated technological solutions and strategic investments. Despite ambitious global targets, McKinsey's projections indicate that global emissions are likely to peak between 2025 and 2035 before beginning to decline. However, these emissions will probably remain above the trajectory required to limit global warming to 1.5°C. 3 This points to a "reality gap" between stated net - zero goals and the concrete actions and technological deployments needed to achieve them, a sentiment echoed in other industry analyses. 5

Successfully navigating this transition involves a delicate balancing act. Energy companies and policymakers must pursue decarbonization while simultaneously ensuring energy affordability, maintaining system reliability, and bolstering energy security, par ticularly in an environment of heightened geopolitical tensions and market uncertainties. 3 As Boston Consulting Group (BCG) noted in 20 24, governments are increasingly prioritizing national security, energy affordability, and economic growth on par with climate objectives. 6  This multi-faceted challenge underscores the complex decision-making environment for energy leaders. As ASUENE emphasized in 20 25, "Decarbonization remains the cornerstone of ESG strategies in the energy sector. Governments and corporations are pushing for cleaner portfolios, targeting net-zero emissions by mid-century or sooner". 1

## Economic, Geopolitical, and Regulatory Influences

The operational landscape for energy companies is further complicated by a confluence of economic, geopolitical, and regulatory factors. Global energy markets are becoming increasingly liberalized, complex, and data - driven. A notable trend is the surge in power purchase agreements (PPAs), largely fueled by corporate demand for renewable energy; European PPA volumes, for example, reached 21 GW in 2024. 8 This shift creates new opportunities for sophisticated energy management and trading platforms.

Market volatility remains a significant concern, intensified by the growing share of intermittent renewable energy sources in the generation mix and the persistent, albeit evolving, role of natural gas in the energy transition. 8 This volatility necessitates advanced forecasting, grid balancing, and risk management technologies.

Regulatory frameworks are continuously evolving to support the energy transition, but they also introduce new layers of complexity for businesses. For instance, the European Union is working to update its Climate Law with the aim to cut emissions by 40% by 2040 and to accelerate the deployment of low - carbon technologies. 7 In North America, landmark policies such as the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) are acting as powerful catalysts, spurring domestic manufacturing and the deployment of clean energy technologies. 9 While not specific to the energy sector, Forrester's 2025 emerging technologies report notes a broader regulatory encouragement for organizations to adopt synthetic data to minimize risk, a trend that could permeate the energy industry as data -driven deci sion-making becomes more critical. 10 The impact of political shifts on energy policy and investment strategies is also undeniable, as highlighted by BCG's observation that "The 2024 elections have redrawn the global energy map... Every country is making trade - offs to balance sustainability w ith economic and political realities". 6

The Rising Importance of ESG in Technology Decisions

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) principles are no longer peripheral concerns but are fundamentally redefining how energy companies operate, invest, and report their performance. 1  This shift has profound implications for technology procurement.

Decarbonization stands as a primary ESG driver, compelling significant investments in renewable energy generation, Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS) technologies, and other emissions-reducing solutions. 1  Social equity considerations, including the critical issues of energy access and affordability for all populations, are also gaining prominence. This focus is influencing technology choices, leading to increased interest in solutions like off-grid solar projects and microgrids designed to serve remote or underserved communities. 1

From a governance perspective, investors are demanding greater transparency, enhanced board accountability, and more robust climate risk management practices. The adoption of reporting frameworks such as the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) and standards from the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) is becoming widespread. ASUENE reported in 20 25 that an estimated 74% of energy companies were aligned with TCFD in 20 24, a substantial increase from 23% in 20 20 . 1  Compliance with these frameworks often necessitates sophisticated data management, analytics, and reporting software, making these technology categories key investment areas. 1

Digitalization is emerging as a critical enabler of ESG performance. As ASUENE stated in 20 25, "Digital tools are playing a critical role in helping energy companies manage ESG targets and regulatory obligations". 1  Specific technologies being deployed include real-time emissions monitoring systems, AI-powered predictive maintenance to reduce operational risks and environmental incidents, and blockchain platforms for transparent carbon credit tracking and energy trading. 1  The financial markets are also reinforcing this trend, with green finance mechanisms and sustainability-linked investments increasingly channeling capital towards ESG-aligned projects and companies. This directly influences technology procurement decisions, as companies seek solutions that can help them meet the criteria for such funding. 1  The strong correlation between robust ESG propositions and higher equity returns, as noted by Zetwerk in 20 24, further underscores the financial incentives driving these technology investments. 12

Regional Context: North America, EMEA, APAC Overviews

The global trends in the energy sector manifest differently across regions, shaped by local policies, resource availability, economic conditions, and societal priorities.

- ● North America: The region is witnessing record levels of investment in the energy transition, with the U.S. deploying $303.3 billion in financing for clean energy technologies in 2023, significantly boosted by policies like the IRA. 9 Key technology investment areas include solar power, energy storage (with 7.5 GW added in the U.S. in 2023), electric vehicles (EVs), Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS), hydrogen, and Renewable Natural Gas (RNG). 9  The U.S. stands as a global leader in power sector digitalization, with numerous projects focused on advanced grid control systems and analytics software. 9 However, challenges persist, particularly in ensuring resource adequacy to meet growing demand (e.g., from data centers), modernizing the grid, and streamlining permitting processes for new energy infrastructure. 13 Deloitte's 2025 outlook for Canada highlighted stable oil prices and robust natural gas demand, with a notable increase in Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) exports. 14
- ● EMEA: In Europe, the B2B energy services market is poised for significant expansion, with projections to reach a value of €100 billion. Electric mobility and hydrogen solutions are identified as the largest growth segments within this market. 15 The European Union continues to drive ambitious climate policies, aiming for substantial emissions cuts and the accelerated deployment of low -carbon technologies. 7 The United Kingdom, for example, has raised its emissions reduction target to 81% by 2035, compared to 1990 levels. 7 Despite these ambitions, an Accenture report from 2024 indicated that only 16% of the world's largest companies, many of which are headquartered or have significant operations in EMEA, are currently on track to meet their net - zero targets, although a high  percentage (85%) have established energy efficiency targets. 2
- ● APAC: The Asia-Pacific region is characterized by rapid growth in energy demand, particularly in emerging economies such as the ASEAN nations and India.  APAC is also at the forefront of EV adoption, with China alone accounting 4 for an estimated 60% of global EV sales in 2024. 17 Key technology priorities for the region, as outlined by APEC for 2025, include expanding clean electricity generation (encompassing renewables, nuclear power, hydrogen, and CCUS), strengthening grid security and undertaking necessary modernization, and leveraging AI-driven innovation in the energy sector. 18 IDC research underscores the energy transition efforts in APAC, focusing on the interplay of national policies, industrial priorities, and the deployment of digital enablers such as AI and digital twins to achieve decarbonization goals. 19 However, the pace of

transition may vary, with some nations like Indonesia potentially moderating their climate ambitions in the short term to prioritize economic growth and ensure energy security. 7

The confluence of these global and regional factors creates a dynamic and demanding environment for the energy sector. The "dual challenge"-meeting rising global energy demand, particularly from developing economies in APAC  3 , while simultaneously accelerating decarbonization efforts  1 -is intensifying. This is not merely about adding renewable capacity but involves a fundamental re-engineering of the entire energy system. This re-engineering must occur under increasing economic pressures, geopolitical uncertainties, and evolving societal expectations, as noted by McKinsey's observation of rising costs and complexity in the transition's new phase  3 , and BCG's emphasis on governments balancing multiple priorities. 6 Technology providers must therefore develop solutions that address both the efficiency and reliability of existing energy systems and the enablement of new, cleaner energy paradigms.

Furthermore, ESG considerations are rapidly evolving from a compliance-focused reporting exercise to a core strategic and operational driver, directly shaping technology choices. The ESG trends outlined by ASUENE-decarbonization, energy access, governance, digitalization for ESG, and green finance  1 -all have direct technological underpinnings. For example, achieving decarbonization targets necessitates investment in renewable energy technologies, while robust ESG performance monitoring relies on advanced AI and data analytics. The financial incentives are also clear, with strong ESG propositions correlating with higher equity returns and reduced risk. 12  Given that a significant portion of large firms are still not on track for net-zero  2 , the impetus for more aggressive action and, consequently, greater technology adoption to meet these goals is substantial. Technology procurement is thus increasingly viewed through an ESG lens, where the assessment extends beyond a technology's functional capabilities to its contribution to the company's overall ESG performance and targets. This creates distinct opportunities for vendors who can clearly articulate and quantify the ESG benefits embedded in their solutions.

## 2. Technology Investment Priorities

Energy sector organizations are making substantial investments in a diverse array of technologies to navigate the energy transition, enhance operational efficiency, meet evolving customer demands, and adhere to stringent regulatory and ESG requirements. These investments span foundational digital transformation pillars, advanced AI applications, critical cybersecurity measures, and specialized technologies for decarbonization and new energy systems.

## Core Digital Transformation Pillars (Cloud, Data Analytics, IoT)

Digital transformation remains a paramount priority for energy companies. This is particularly evident in the downstream oil and gas sub - sector, where initiatives aim to improve operational efficiencies, enhance customer experience, and ensure business continuity. 21 Although some data points to a lag in progress, the foundational need for these capabilities is undisputed. Accenture's 2024 analysis underscores the importance of prioritizing and strengthening the "digital core." This involves establishing an effective  data foundation and adopting a cloud - first infrastructure, which are seen as crucial enablers for leveraging advanced technologies like Artificial Intelligence. 22

The Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) is another key area. IDC's 2025 research highlights that IIoT platforms and applications are vital for enabling data - driven decision-making, implementing predictive maintenance strategies, and achieving enhanced ope rational efficiency across the energy value chain. 19 This move towards interconnected and intelligent operational environments is a significant shift. Reinforcing this, Gartner's 2025 Market Guide for Renewable Energy Management Solutions notes an accelerating transition within the renewable energy industry from traditional, siloed operational systems to a new generation of integrated, cloud - based solutions. 23 This migration to the cloud facilitates greater scalability, flexibility, and accessibility of data and applications.

## The AI Revolution in Energy: From Operations to Customer Experience

Artificial Intelligence, encompassing Generative AI (GenAI) and the emerging field of agentic AI, is poised to bring transformative changes to the energy sector. These technologies are expected to drive innovation, significantly enhance operational capabilities, and optimize resource management across the board. 2 Forrester identified Agentic AI, which enables systems to make decisions and act independently, as one of the top emerging technologies for 2025. 10

Accenture's Technology Vision 2025 further elaborates on this, highlighting that AI agents are set to revolutionize how enterprises operate. A significant 85% of energy executives surveyed by Accenture agree that AI agents will fundamentally reinvent the construction of digital systems. 24  As a practical example, Repsol, a global multienergy company, is extending its co-innovation agreement with Accenture to accelerate the adoption of GenAI across its operations, aiming for enhanced process efficiency in areas like planning, forecasting, and application maintenance. 24

The applications of AI in the energy sector are diverse and impactful. They include predictive analytics for optimizing energy distribution and improving energy efficiency, predictive failure detection for critical assets, comprehensive asset performance management, and even pioneering applications like autonomous drilling. Notably, SLB and Equinor achieved a milestone in 20 24 by drilling the most autonomous well section to date, leveraging AI-driven technologies for surface automation and directional drilling. 24  IDC research also indicates that AI and GenAI are taking center stage in the net-zero technology investment strategies of global oil and gas leaders. 19

However, this AI revolution is not without its challenges. A significant concern is the substantial energy demand of AI-centric data centers. Gartner projects that energy demands specifically linked to GenAI in data centers will grow by a staggering 160 % in the next couple of years. This surge could potentially lead to power shortages affecting 40 % of AI-focused data centers by 20 27. 25  This creates a complex interplay: the energy sector increasingly relies on AI for optimization and transition, while simultaneously facing the challenge of providing the vast amounts of clean and reliable power that the AI infrastructure itself requires.

## Cybersecurity in a Connected Energy Ecosystem

As the energy sector's infrastructure becomes increasingly digitized and interconnected through technologies like smart grids and IIoT, cybersecurity has escalated to a paramount concern. 26 The operational technology (OT) and information technology (IT) environments are converging, expanding the potential attack surface for malicious actors.

Accenture identifies "Data and infrastructure security" as a critical area for reinventing the energy sector, emphasizing that "Cyber threats targeting energy companies are relentless, from pipeline controls to drilling operations". 28 This highlights the vulnerability of critical energy infrastructure. Globally, Gartner forecasts a significant 15% rise in cybersecurity spending in 2025, with expenditures expected to reach $212 billion. North America is anticipated to continue dominatin

this spending category. 27 Cybersecurity considerations in the energy sector are twofold: they apply to the security of product development processes (e.g., protecting intellectual property related to new energy technologies) and, critically, to the operational security of connected products and systems once deployed in the fie ld. 26

## Key Technologies for Decarbonization and New Energy (REMS, Storage, Hydrogen, CCUS, Grid Modernization)

A significant portion of technology investment is directed towards solutions that directly support decarbonization and the integration of new energy sources.

- ● Renewable Energy Management Solutions (REMS): The adoption of sophisticated REMS is accelerating rapidly. These platforms are essential for managing diverse renewable energy portfolios, including solar, wind, Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), and hybrid assets. Gartner predicts a major shift in this area, stating that "By 2027, 35% of fragmented legacy systems for managing renewable assets will be replaced by renewable energy generators, enhancing efficiency and reducing operational risk". 23
- ● Energy Storage: Energy storage technologies, particularly BESS, are critical for ensuring grid stability and enabling the consistent supply of renewable energy, which is often intermittent. The U.S. market saw substantial growth in energy storage in 2024, with a record 1 1.9 GW of new capacity added across grid -scale and distributed applications. 29 As Plexus highlighted in 2024, "energy storage technology and its wider adoption remains a critical enabler to achieving net zero ambitions". 26
- ● Hydrogen:  Green hydrogen is increasingly viewed as a key vector for decarbonizing hard -to-abate sectors, and investments in its production and use are growing. 1 Deloitte's 2024 outlook noted that clean hydrogen is "teed up for takeoff". 30 However, McKinsey's 2024 Global Energy Perspective introduced a note of caution, suggesting that hydrogen demand might be up to 25% lower than previously anticipated due to rising production costs and ongoing regulatory uncertainties. 16
- ● Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS): CCUS technologies are being increasingly deployed as a means to mitigate emissions from industrial processes and, in some cases, directly from the atmosphere. 1 In the U.S., new investments in CCS projects totaled $2.8 billion in 2023. 9
- ● Grid Modernization: The modernization of electricity grids is fundamental to the energy transition. Enhanced grid infrastructure is essential for integrating large

volumes of renewable energy at scale, managing bidirectional power flows, and ensuring a stable and resilient supply. The International Energy Agency (IEA) estimated in 20 23 that over 80  million kilometers of grid lines need to be added or upgraded globally by 20 40  to meet transition needs. 18  Smart grid technologies are recognized as a top ESG-enabling technology, as they help reduce transmission losses and facilitate the seamless integration of renewables. 1  The U.S. is notably a global leader in power sector digitalization, with a strong focus on grid control systems and advanced analytics. 9

- ● Renewable Fuels:  The demand for renewable fuels, such as Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) and renewable diesel, is projected to increase significantly. Bain &amp; Company projects that demand could be two to four times higher by 2050, creating a potential profit pool of $100 b illion to $150 billion. 31

## Regional Spotlights: Divergent and Convergent Tech Priorities

While there are overarching global technology trends, specific priorities and the pace of adoption vary by region.

- ● North America: The region exhibits a strong focus on deploying solar power and energy storage, with a record 11.9 GW of storage added in 2024. 29 Investments in EVs, CCS (with $2.8 billion in new investments in 2023  9 ), and hydrogen (with $1.9 billion invested in 2023 9 ) are also significant. Grid digitalization is a major theme. 9 Despite the growth in renewables, natural gas remained the largest source of electricity generation in the U.S. in 2024, accounting for 42.9% of the total output. 29 The increasing power demand from data centers is a notable driver for new generation capacity in the region. 8
- ● EMEA: In Europe, electric mobility and hydrogen solutions are emerging as major growth markets for B2B energy services. 15 The electrification of transport is a key trend, with electric buses, for example, gaining considerable traction (representing 16% of new bus sales in the EU in the first half of 2024 32 ). Crossindustry collaboration is deemed essential for decarbonizing heavy industrial sectors. 32
- ● APAC: The Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) has outlined key energy priorities for 2025, which include expanding clean electricity generation (from sources like renewables, nuclear, hydrogen, and CCUS), strengthening grid security through modernization, and fostering AI -driven energy innovation. 18 China is a dominant force in the region, leading global EV sales and pioneering advanced EV infrastructure, including battery swapping technology. 17 The region is also seeing innovation in energy -efficient AI chips and advanced cooling

solutions for data centers, such as immersion cooling. 17 IDC research highlights that APAC's energy transition strategy focuses on deploying clean technologies alongside digital enablers like AI and digital twins. 19

The interplay between AI and the energy sector presents a particularly noteworthy dynamic. AI is undeniably a critical enabler for optimizing complex energy systems, enhancing operational efficiency, and managing the increasing penetration of variable renewable energy sources. 10  Gartner explicitly points to AI's role in optimizing energy efficiency and integrating diverse renewable sources through predictive analytics. 23 However, this symbiotic relationship is also strained by the immense power consumption of AI itself, especially GenAI-driven data centers. Gartner's warning that these data centers' energy demand could grow 160 % in a few years, potentially outstripping power supply for 40 % of AI data centers by 20 27, is stark. 25 McKinsey further projects that data centers driven by AI could account for 5-9% of global electricity consumption by 20 50 . 4  This creates a compelling feedback loop: the energy sector must rapidly scale up its generation capacity and accelerate decarbonization efforts to power the AI revolution, while simultaneously relying heavily on AI-driven technologies to achieve these ambitious scaling and decarbonization goals. This presents a dual opportunity for technology vendors: offering AI solutions for energy optimization and providing energy-efficient AI infrastructure and solutions.

Furthermore, while the energy sector universally recognizes the imperative of digital transformation-embracing cloud computing, advanced data analytics, IoT, and AIsignificant execution gaps persist. Forrester's 20 22 study with DTN, though slightly predating the 20 23-20 25 focus, indicated that while digital modernization was a top priority, 43% of downstream oil and gas companies had not yet begun their initiatives, and a key impediment was the inability to effectively leverage operational data. 21 These foundational challenges likely persist. Accenture's 20 24 analysis reinforces this by stressing the ongoing need for a strong "digital core," comprising a robust data foundation and cloud infrastructure, to truly enable AI advancements. 22  IDC similarly points to the crucial role of IIoT in enabling data-driven decision-making and predictive maintenance. 19  Compounding these challenges is the escalating cybersecurity threat landscape that accompanies increased connectivity. 26 Consequently, there is a substantial market opportunity for technology vendors whose solutions not only provide advanced capabilities but also pragmatically address the practical challenges of data integration from disparate sources, ensuring data quality, modernizing legacy systems, and implementing robust cybersecurity measures within complex and often critical energy environments. Solutions that are readily implementable and demonstrate a clear return on investment by addressing these foundational gaps will be highly valued.

## 3. Buyer Mindset by Role

Understanding the diverse perspectives and motivations within the energy technology buying committee is essential for effective B2B marketing and sales. Decisions are rarely made by a single individual; rather, they are the result of a complex interplay be tween various stakeholders, each with distinct priorities and concerns.

## The Modern Energy Tech Buying Committee: Key Personas and Their Motivations

B2B technology procurement in the energy sector, as in other complex industries, involves an increasingly multifaceted buying committee. Globally, this committee now averages 10-11 individuals. 3  In the APAC region, this number can be even larger, with 3 an average of 13 individuals involved, often spanning two or more departments. 34 Key personas typically include:

- ● IT Leadership (CIOs, IT Directors, IT Managers):
- Their primary focus is on strengthening the organization's digital core capabilities, enabling advanced AI enhancements, and ensuring a robust data strategy supported by a cloud - first infrastructure.22 They are deeply concerned with overarching issues such  as cybersecurity, data governance, the seamless integration of new technologies with existing legacy systems, and the effective management of IT budgets.27 A significant challenge for IT leadership is the phenomenon of "shadow IT" -technology solutions pro cured and implemented by business units without formal IT approval. CIOs are increasingly tasked with

developing strategies to harness the innovative potential of shadow IT while mitigating associated risks related to cost, security, and compatibility.35 Their goal is to foster an environment where business-led innovation can flourish without compromising enterprise-wide security, compliance, or operational efficiency.35

- ● Line of Business (LoB) Leaders (Heads of Operations, Engineering, Production, Sustainability):

These leaders are driven by tangible operational outcomes. Their key motivators include improving operational efficiency, maximizing asset performance and uptime, optimizing production processes, ensuring workplace safety, and achieving corporate sustainability targets.1 They actively seek technologies that can directly address their specific pain points and deliver measurable results. Examples include AI for predictive maintenance to reduce downtime, REMS for optimizing the output of renewable energy assets, and IIoT solutions for enhanced operational visibility and control.19 According to IDC, the drive for speed and specific results often leads business teams to procure technology solutions independently (shadow IT), sometimes perceiving centralized IT processes as an impediment.35

- ● C-Suite Executives (CEO, CFO, COO):

The C-suite maintains a strategic overview, prioritizing overall business resilience, sustainable growth, profitability, effective cost management, and the achievement of long-term strategic objectives such as the energy transition and net-zero emissions targets.22 Accenture's 20 24 research indicates that C-level le a de rs will increasingly focus on leveraging AI to improve operational resilience and overall effectiveness in the coming years.28 They are particularly concerned with prudent capital allocation, ensuring a strong return on investment (ROI) for all te chnology expenditures, and ultimately delivering shareholder value.36 A 20 25 Bain survey of Energy and Natural Resources (ENR) executives revealed that many are facing tighter budgets and rapidly rising capital costs, compelling them to make difficult decisions about where to place their strategic bets.38

- ● ESG and Sustainability Officers:

This role is rapidly gaining influence within energy organizations and, consequently, in technology procurement decisions. Their primary responsibility is to ensure that all operations and investments, including technology adoption, align with corporate sustainability goals, emissions reduction targets, and evolving regulatory compliance mandates.1 They champion investments in technologies that support emissions monitoring and reporting, facilitate the integration of renewable energy sources, enhance energy efficiency across operations, and promote circular economy initiatives.1 A key focus for ESG officers is the verifiability and data-backed proof of ESG performance, making technologies that offer robust tracking and reporting capabilities particularly attractive.1

## Navigating Internal Dynamics: IT vs. Business - Led Procurement, Shadow IT

The emergence and growth of "shadow IT" represent a significant internal dynamic influencing technology procurement. This trend involves business units independently procuring and implementing technology solutions, often fueled by decentralized budgetary c ontrol and the ease of access to cloud - based services. 35 As noted by IDC in 2025, this behavior frequently stems from the pressing need of Line of Business (LoB) departments for speed and highly specific solutions, coupled with a perception that traditional IT procurement processes can be slow or unresponsive t o their immediate requirements. 35

While shadow IT can be a valuable source of innovation and agility, allowing teams to quickly address specific challenges, it also introduces a range of potential risks. These include increased overall costs due to missed opportunities for volume discounts  or enterprise agreements, heightened security and compliance vulnerabilities if solutions are not properly vetted, the proliferation of redundant or incompatible systems leading to data silos, and the risk of vendor lock - in if contracts are negotiated wit hout sufficient technical or commercial expertise. 35

IDC advises that rather than attempting to block all non - standard technology purchases, which can stifle innovation, CIOs should proactively partner with LoB leaders. The goal is to regulate and leverage shadow IT, transforming it from an uncontrolled risk  into a structured, business -aligned innovation strategy. 35 This can involve creating curated internal IT marketplaces that offer a pre - approved list of SaaS solutions, third-party tools, and integration - friendly alternatives, thereby providing business units with a faster, sanctioned route to acquiring needed technologies while ensuring security, compliance, and cost - efficiency. The IDC Energy Insights research and advisory service is specifically designed to support both utility IT and business leadership in navigating these complex strategic technology decisions. 39

Impact of Generational Shifts and Digital Savviness (esp. APAC)

For instance, 15% of Gen Z buyers reported using AI "a lot" in their procurement research, nearly double the 8% average across all surveyed buyers. Furthermore, 30 % of Gen Z buyers stated they "always" or "very often" trust AI-generated content, compared to 20 % of all buyers surveyed. 42  This demographic evolution necessitates a fundamental reassessment of traditional B2B marketing and sales strategies to effectively engage this technologically sophisticated and values-driven audience. 40

The increasing empowerment of LoB buyers, often driven by the accessibility of cloud solutions and a desire for agility, is leading to a "democratization of tech procurement." Technology decisions are becoming less centralized within traditional IT departments. LoB leaders, particularly those from younger, more digitally fluent demographics prevalent in regions like APAC, are more self-sufficient in researching, evaluating, and even procuring solutions that meet their specific needs. 35  This trend, where Gartner notes 75% of B2B buyers prefer a rep-free experience for much of their journey  43 , a nd where buying committees are large and functionally diverse  33 , requires a significant shift in vendor engagement strategies. Technology providers can no longer rely solely on targeting IT departments as the primary gateway. They must develop nuanced approaches to identify, engage, and influence a broader spectrum of stakeholders, including key LoB decision-makers and functional heads like  sustainability officers, often much earlier in their independent research process. This engagement must be tailored to their unique pain points, speak their functional language, and align with their specific strategic objectives.

Concurrently, ESG is transitioning from a niche concern to a "table stakes"

consideration across multiple buyer roles. While dedicated ESG officers are increasingly common, the expectation that technology solutions will support broader sustainability goals is permeating the mindsets of IT, LoB, and C-suite executives alike. ASUENE's research highlights multiple ESG trends-decarbonization, energy access, governance, digitalization for ESG-all directly impacting technology choices in the energy sector. 1  C-suite executives are accountable for achieving net-zero targets and navigating the energy transition. 22  LoB leaders in operations are intrinsically concerned with efficiency and resource optimization, which naturally align with the environmental aspects of ESG. 23  IT leaders are responsible for implementing the systems necessary for robust ESG data management and transparent reporting. 1 Adding to this, the growing cohort of younger buyers, particularly in APAC, actively prioritizes vendors with strong ESG credentials. 41  Therefore, technology vendors must compellingly demonstrate how their solutions contribute to overarching ESG objectives relevant to each stakeholder within the buying committee. A solution's ability to verifiably reduce emissions, improve resource efficiency, or enhance transparency can serve as a significant differentiator and a key factor in the decisionmaking process across various buyer roles.

## 4. Messaging and Content Preferences

In the contemporary B2B technology landscape, particularly within the dynamic energy sector, buyers are increasingly self - reliant and discerning. Their preferences for messaging and content are shifting away from traditional sales pitches towards valuable, credible, and easily digestible information that aids their independent decision-making process.

## Content That Resonates: Thought Leadership, Case Studies, Data - Backed Insights

B2B buyers engaged in complex technology purchases within the energy sector are predominantly self -directed and actively seek content that is credible, solution -focused, and provides genuine insights. 33 Generic marketing collateral is often overlooked in favor of materials that demonstrate a deep understanding of their challenges and offer tangible solutions. "Thought leadership content shared by reputable market figures (like journalists)" is particularly valued, as it provides an external, often more objective, perspective. 33

Genuine case studies and detailed industry reports that highlight measurable wins and quantifiable outcomes are crucial for building credibility and demonstrating a vendor's capability. 33 For example, companies like Siemens and Schneider Electric effectively utilize case studies to showcase the impact of their sustainability initiatives and technological solutions in real-world scenarios, thereby building trust and illustrating value. 47

Data-backed insights are essential to capture and retain the attention of technically astute and ROI-focused buyers in the energy sector. Content that includes peer benchmarking data, industry statistics, and evidence-based analysis can effectively pique buyers' interest and encourage them to consider third-party perspectives. 43 According to TrustRadius's 20 25 research, enterprise buyers consistently consult several key resources during their evaluation process: product demos (55%), their own prior experience with a vendor or technology (54%), the vendor's product website (43%), and user reviews (43%). 42  This underscores the importance of providing comprehensive, transparent, and easily accessible information through multiple channels.

## The Role of Vendor - Owned Channels (Websites, Demos) vs. Third - Party Validation (Reviews, Analyst Reports)

While vendor-owned channels play a vital role, a balanced approach that incorporates third - party validation is critical for earning buyer trust. Vendor and product websites (consulted by 43% of enterprise buyers) and product demonstrations (55%) are founda tional resources that buyers actively seek out. 42 These channels offer vendors direct control over messaging and the opportunity to provide in -depth information.

However, as TodayDigital aptly noted in 2025, "owned media isn't enough - other, reputable people have to vouch for you". 33 This is where third -party validation becomes indispensable. User reviews have emerged as a highly influential resource, consulted by 43% of enterprise buyers 42 , with an even broader 77% of all B2B buyers checking user reviews before committing to a software purchase. 46 The relatability of reviewers-in terms of company size, industry, and role -is a significant factor in the perceived value of these reviews. 42

Conversely, the influence of traditional analyst reports is experiencing a notable decline. TrustRadius research from 20 25 revealed that only 14% of technology buyers now use analyst reports in their decision-making process, marking a substantial 60 % drop since 20 22. 46 This decline may be attributed to a perception among buyers that some analyst reports can be influenced by vendor relationships ("pay-to-play"), thereby diminishing their perceived objectivity.

In this evolving landscape, external influencers-including industry journalists, podcasters, and niche subject matter experts-are gaining prominence in shaping B2B buyer decisions. Forrester's research indicates that 30 % of Millennial and Gen Z buyers, a growing demographic in the workforce, include ten or more such external influencers in their buying processes. 49

## Messaging Themes: Value, ROI, Sustainability, Innovation, Reliability, Security

Effective messaging in the energy technology market must be grounded in a deep understanding of buyer needs and priorities, moving beyond mere product capabilities to address core business challenges and aspirations. 43 Forrester advises a shift in approach: "Help  - don't just sell". 49

- ● Value &amp; ROI:  Buyers are looking for partners who understand their challenges and can deliver demonstrable value that extends beyond the initial sale. 49 While price may not always be the primary shortlisting factor, particularly in the APAC region, it invariably becomes a critical consideration as negotiations progress. A clear articulation of Return on Investment (ROI) is a cornerstone of purchasing decisions. 34
- ● Sustainability: The importance of sustainability in purchasing decisions is rapidly increasing, driven by corporate commitments, regulatory pressures, and the values of younger buyer demographics, especially in APAC. 41 Companies like Siemens and Applied Materials are often preferred due to their demonstrated leadership in sustainability. 47 Messaging related to sustainability must be authentic, transparent, and meticulously avoid any semblance of "greenwashing". 47
- ● Innovation: Highlighting innovative solutions, particularly those leveraging AI and other emerging technologies, is key to capturing buyer interest. 10 However, it is crucial to emphasize the practical applications and tangible benefits of these innovations rather than relying on generalized hype. 27
- ● Reliability &amp; Security: Given the critical nature of energy operations and infrastructure, reliability and security are non - negotiable requirements. 1

Cybersecurity, in particular, is a major concern for energy companies, and solutions that address these vulnerabilities will resonate strongly. 26

- ● Transparency: Building trust with modern B2B buyers necessitates transparency. Vendors should be open about both the strengths and limitations of their offerings. 33 Digitalization enables verifiability, which is crucial as ESG assurance becomes standard, reinforcing the need for transparent, data - backed sustainability messaging. 1

## Tailoring Content for Different Buying Stages and Roles

To maximize impact, content must be carefully tailored to the specific stage of the buyer's journey and the distinct roles and motivations of the individuals within the buying committee. Gartner's B2B Buying Journey model, which outlines six key stages-Problem Identification, Solution Exploration, Requirements Building, Supplier Selection, Validation, and Consensus Creation -provides a valuable framework for this. 50

- ● Awareness/Problem Identification: At this early stage, buyers are recognizing a business issue or opportunity. Effective content includes blog posts, ebooks, whitepapers, and webinars designed to help them understand their challenges and explore potential avenues for solutions. 44 Thought leadership content that frames the problem and offers high - level insights is particularly effective here. 33
- ● Consideration/Solution Exploration: As buyers begin to research potential solutions, content such as product - focused webinars, detailed comparison guides, and initial case studies can help position a vendor's expertise and offerings. 44 Vendor websites and interactive product demos become increasingly important resources at this stage. 42
- ● Decision/Validation: When buyers have narrowed their choices and are ready to make a decision, content that provides deep validation is critical. This includes personalized consultations, industry - specific case studies demonstrating proven results, customer testimonials, in -depth product demos, and free trials or pilot programs. 44  Authentic user reviews and peer conversations play a vital role in this final validation phase. 42

Beyond journey stage, content must also be nuanced to address the specific concerns of different members of the buying group. For example, technical specifications and performance data will be crucial for engineering leads, while ROI calculations and busin ess impact analyses will resonate more with finance executives. Sustainability impact reports will be key for ESG officers. As Mavis Liew from

Forrester (APAC) advises, "B2B buying always occurs as a buying group -  s o ide ntify who the buying group members are, what their buying role is... and what those motivations are... By effectively intimately understanding each persona in the buying group, communication can be tailored...". 34

## Regional Content Nuances: Language, Cultural Relevance, Channel Preferences

Content strategies must also account for significant regional variations in language, cultural norms, and channel preferences.

- ● APAC: Cultural nuances are paramount in APAC. Business etiquette, communication styles, and buyer expectations vary widely across countries such as Japan (valuing indirect communication and harmony), Australia (favoring directness and informality), China (empha sizing relationships or "guanxi"), and India (respecting hierarchy and seniority). 34 Consequently, messaging must be meticulously localized, and sales approaches adapted accordingly. APAC buyers, often younger, also place a higher priority on DEI and ESG initiatives than their global peers. 41 They are the most frequent encounters of Google's AI Overviews in search results (84%), indicating a high reliance on digital research channels. 42
- ● EMEA: Buyers in the EMEA region encounter Google's AI Overviews less frequently (59%) than in other regions. 42 The B2B energy services market in Europe is experiencing robust growth, necessitating content that focuses on solutions for energy efficiency, on - site generation, EV charging infrastructure, hydrogen applications, and CCUS for industrial customers. 15
- ● North America: U.S. - based buyers fall in the middle regarding encounters with Google AI Overviews (73%). 42  Content strategies should address specific North American priorities, such as the impacts of the Inflation Reduction Act, the challenges of grid modernization, and the escalating energy needs of data centers. 9 Given that North America dominates global cybersecurity spending, content focused on robust security solutions is likely to find a receptive audience. 27

The increasing skepticism of B2B tech buyers, particularly in the intricate energy sector, signals a critical need for marketers to shift from traditional persuasion tactics to a strategy rooted in substantiation. Buyers are self - directing their research a nd place greater trust in peer validation and demonstrable proof points rather than unverified vendor claims. 33 The significant decline in the use of traditional analyst reports 46 underscores this trust deficit. Instead, buyers are turning to resources like user reviews (43%), their own prior experiences (54%), and detailed product demos

(55%). 42 They actively seek "genuine case studies and industry reports highlighting measurable wins". 33  This environment demands that marketers prioritize the creation and dissemination of a comprehensive repository of credible, transparent, and easily accessible proof points. This includes investing in robust customer advocacy programs to generate authentic reviews and in-depth case studies, providing interactive and detailed product demonstrations, and ensuring that all website content is factual, data-rich, and directly addresses buyer concerns. The emphasis must be unequivocally on showing tangible value and performance, not merely telling a persuasive story.

While tailoring content to specific roles within the buying committee remains important, the evolving buyer landscape calls for a deeper level of personalization that addresses "value alignment" across the entire diverse buying group. The increasing emphasis on overarching values, such as ESG and DEI (particularly among younger buyers in APAC  41 ), combined with the complexity of modern technology solutions, means that messaging must demonstrate how a particular technology aligns with the collective values and strategic objectives of the organization, not just the functional requirements of individual departments or roles. Gartner's inclusion of "Consensus Creation" as a critical final stage in the B2B buying journey 50 highlights this need. Furthermore, with organizational changes driving 99% of B2B purchases 43 , solutions must be positioned as addressing broader, longer - term strategic challenges. Therefore, content needs to articulate a multi - faceted value proposition. For instance, a smart grid solution should be shown to not only improve operational efficiency  for the Head of Operations and reduce costs for the CFO, but also demonstrably support corporate net - zero goals for the CEO and Sustainability Lead, and enhance grid reliability for the broader community, thus addressing wider ESG concerns. This requires  a sophisticated understanding of the target organization's overarching strategic priorities and an ability to weave these into a cohesive and compelling narrative that resonates across the entire buying committee.

## 5. Buying Journey &amp; Channel Behavior

The way B2B technology buyers in the energy sector research, evaluate, and purchase solutions has undergone a significant transformation. Understanding this evolving journey and the channels they prefer is critical for vendors to align their sales and marketing efforts effectively.

## The Self - Directed B2B Technology Buyer in Energy

A dominant trend across the B2B landscape, including the energy sector, is the rise of the self - directed buyer. Globally, research indicates that buyers are initiating contact with vendors in as many as 81% of cases, rather than responding to outbound sale s efforts. 33 This marks a fundamental power shift in the buyer - seller dynamic. Gartner's research further reveals that a substantial 75% of B2B buyers now prefer a sales representative -free experience for much of their purchasing journey. They are independently conduc ting extensive research, comparing pricing and solutions, reading peer reviews, and often engaging with product trials or demos before formally engaging with a sales team. 43

This shift towards self -direction is influenced by several factors. The pervasiveness of the "Amazon experience" in consumer purchasing has reshaped expectations for B2B interactions, with buyers now anticipating similar levels of autonomy, information accessibility, and ease of use in their professional procurement activities. 51 Additionally, changing work patterns, including more flexible hours and remote work, often lead employees to prefer conducting research at their own pace and convenience, outside of direct salesperson interaction. 51 Technological advancements have also been a key enabler, with the increasing availability, declining cost, and improved ease of implementation of self - service technologies and digital information resources empowering buyers to navigate much of the journey independently. 51

## Mapping the Non - Linear Buying Journey: Key Stages (Gartner's model as a reference)

Contrary to a simplistic linear model, the B2B buying journey in the energy sector is typically complex and non -linear. Buyers frequently revisit earlier stages of their decision-making process, interact with a multitude of touchpoints across various channels, and their engagement patterns can be unpredictable. 45 Sales cycles for significant technology investments in this sector can be lengthy, ranging from a matter of days for smaller purchases to several months or even years for large - scale enterprise solutions. 45 A 20 24 survey indicated that 58% of B2B professionals reported that sales cycles had become even longer, reflecting increased complexity and caution in purchasing decisions. 45

Gartner's six-stage B2B buying journey model-comprising Problem Identification, Solution Exploration, Requirements Building, Supplier Selection, Validation, and Consensus Creation-provides a useful framework for understanding the key phases buyers navigate, even if the progression through these stages is iterative rather than strictly sequential. 43 A significant challenge highlighted by Forrester is that a high percentage of B2B purchases-86%-stall at some point during the buying process, often leading to frustration for both buyers and sellers. 34  Furthermore, Gartner's research indicates that organizational changes are the primary driver for 99% of B2B purchases, suggesting that buyers are typically motivated to solve longer-term, strategic internal challenges that often span multiple departments or functional areas within their organization. 43 This underscores the need for solutions that address broad organizational needs rather than isolated problems.

## Channel Preferences: Digital (Search, Social, AI tools) vs. Human (Sales Reps, Events)

While B2B buyers increasingly prefer self -service and digital channels for research and initial evaluation, human interactions remain crucial at specific points in the journey, particularly for complex, high - value purchases. Research from Gartner suggests that a hybrid approach, combining digital and human touchpoints, is often the most effective. B2B buyers are reportedly 1.8 times more likely to complete a high-quality deal (one that meets their needs with minimal regret) when they engage with supplier -provided digital tools in partnership with a sales representative, rather than using those tools independently or relying solely on sales interactions. 43

## ● Digital Channels:

- ○ Search Engines:  Search, including the increasing presence of AI -generated summaries like Google's AI Overviews, plays a dominant role in how buyers discover information and vendors. 42 TrustRadius reported in 2025 that 72% of B2B buyers encounter these AI Overviews during their research.
- ○ Vendor Websites and Product Demos: These owned digital assets are key resources. 43% of enterprise buyers consult vendor/product websites, and 55% utilize product demos. 42
- ○ User Review Platforms: Independent user review sites have become critical sources of validation and social proof. 42
- ○ Social Media and External Influencers: Social platforms and the content shared by credible external influencers (such as industry journalists, podcasters, and niche experts) are increasingly shaping buyer perceptions and decisions, particularly among younger demographics. 49

## ● Human Channels:

- ○ Vendor Sales Representatives: Despite the preference for self - service, vendor sales reps are still consulted by 41% of enterprise buyers during their decision-making process. 42
- ○ Purpose of Human Interaction: Buyers typically seek human interaction when they need to ask detailed or nuanced questions, understand a vendor's future product roadmap, assess intangible factors like company culture or service quality, explore negotiation options (especially for famil iar products), or gain personalized guidance and expert consultation (particularly for unfamiliar or highly complex solutions). 43
- ○ B2B Events:  While traditionally important, the role of B2B events is evolving. Forrester noted in 2025 that AI has the potential to reshape event planning and execution, but current uptake of AI in this area is low. Event teams also face ongoing challenges related to volatile economic and political climates, impacting budgets and attendance. 52

## The Influence of AI on Buyer Research and Decision - Making

Artificial Intelligence is profoundly reshaping the B2B technology buying journey in 2025. 42 Buyers are increasingly encountering AI -generated content, most notably through search engine features like Google's AI Overviews, which 72% of buyers now report seeing in their search results. 42

The use of AI tools in the buying process is also on the rise. Occasional AI use among buyers increased from 17% in 2024 to 30% in 2025. Furthermore, buyer trust in AI tools is growing, with 80% of buyers reporting that they trust AI tools at least sometimes, a notable 19% increase year-over-year. 42  A significant 40% of buyers state that AI makes it easier for them to find the information they need, a figure that doubled from 2024. 42 As previously mentioned, Gen Z buyers are at the forefront of this trend, demonstrating higher usage and trust in AI tools compared to other demographics. 42

However, this adoption is not without caveats. Even frequent users of AI tools exhibit a degree of skepticism; 62% of these users report that they always or very often fact - check information obtained from AI sources. A striking 90% of buyers who encounter AI Overviews in search results click through to the underlying source documents for verification. 42 This behavior underscores the continued importance of original, credible source content and the need for buyers to validate AI-generated summaries.

## Regional GTM Patterns: Adapting to Local Buying Customs

Go-to-Market (GTM) strategies must be carefully adapted to the distinct buying customs, channel preferences, and market conditions prevalent in different regions.

- ● North America: Buyers in this region, particularly in the U.S., demonstrate a strong comfort level with digital buying experiences, especially for products or services with which they have some familiarity (64% prefer a 100% digital experience in such cases). 43  The high level of cybersecurity spending in North America suggests a receptive market for GTM campaigns focused on security solutions. 27 Messaging should resonate with regional priorities such as efficiency, reliability, and addressing new energy demands stemming from sources like data centers. 13 U.S. - based buyers encounter Google AI Overviews at a rate of 73%. 42
- ● EMEA: The B2B energy services market in Europe is experiencing significant growth, requiring GTM strategies tailored to specific industrial needs, including energy efficiency, on -site generation, EV charging solutions, and hydrogen applications. 15 Sales organizations in this region need to be adept at identifying and targeting the most attractive industries and customer segments. 15 Collaboration is often a key theme, particularly for complex decarbonization solutions. 32 Buyers in EMEA encounter AI-generated search overviews least often (59%) compared to other major regions. 42
- ● APAC: GTM strategies for the APAC region must be highly localized due to the profound diversity in cultures, business practices, and languages across its constituent countries. 34 Buying groups in APAC tend to be larger than in other regions, and external stakeholders, such as consultants and local partners, often play a more significant role in the decision - making process. 41 Brand loyalty is generally weaker in APAC, with only 35% of buyers starting their purchase journey with a single vendor in mind, compared to 45% in North America. 41 This necessitates continuous brand visibility and engagement efforts. APAC buyers reportedly average around 1,300 interactions with various vendors before making a final decision, indicating a longer and more intensive evaluation process. 33 The buyer demographic in APAC skews younger; these buyers are digital - first, AIsavvy, and place a higher emphasis on ESG and DEI considerations. 40  While pricing becomes a critical factor later in the sales cycle, initial shortlisting is often

based on capability and value. 34 APAC buyers have the highest encounter rate with AI Overviews in search (84%). 42

The modern B2B buyer, armed with unprecedented access to information and increasingly comfortable with digital research tools, presents an "informed but overwhelmed" paradox. While AI assistance is making information discovery more efficient for many  42 , the sheer volume of choices, the complexity of solutions, the involvement of large buying committees (10 -13 people  33 ), and the non-linear nature of their journeys can lead to decision fatigue and stalled deals. Forrester's finding that 86% of B2B purchases stall  34 , and that 81% of APAC buyers express dissatisfaction with their chosen providers post-purchase  34 , suggests that simply being "informed" does not equate to an optimal or efficient buying experience. In this environment, the role of technology vendors must evolve from being mere information providers to becoming "sense-makers" or "curators." The challenge is not just to be discoverable, but to actively help buyers navigate complexity, simplify their choices, build consensus within their organizations efficiently, and ultimately make confident decisions. Content and engagement strategies should aim to guide and clarify, providing clear pathways through the information labyrinth, rather than simply adding to the existing noise.

Furthermore, AI is proving to be a double-edged sword in the buying journey. While tools like generative AI search overviews are undoubtedly increasing research efficiency for a significant portion of buyers  42 , a n inherent trust deficit remains. The fact that 80 % of buyers trust AI tools only "at least sometimes"  42 , and that even frequent AI users (62%) diligently fact-check AI-generated information, with 90 % clicking through AI Overview sources for verification  42 , is telling. Concerns about the up-to-dateness of AI models' knowledge bases and the overall newness of the technology contribute to this cautious approach. 42  This implies that while leveraging AI for content discovery and initial buyer engagement is rapidly becoming standard practice, vendors who rely solely on AI-driven interactions or unverified AI-generated content risk eroding buyer trust. The human element, or at least easily verifiable human-validated content and clear pathways to human expertise, remains crucial, particularly for complex validation stages and for building the consensus required for high-value deals. Gartner's finding that B2B buyers are 1.8 times more likely to complete a high-quality deal when engaging in a hybrid human-digital approach reinforces this. 43

## 6. Strategic Insights for Marketers

To effectively engage B2B technology buyers in the evolving energy sector, marketers must adopt strategies that acknowledge the buyer's increasing autonomy, address their demand for credible information, and navigate the complexities of multi -stakeholder d ecision-making. The following strategic imperatives can guide marketers in this dynamic environment.

## Building Credibility and Trust in a Skeptical Market

In an era where B2B buyers are largely self-directing their research and exhibit a healthy skepticism towards traditional vendor - led conversations, establishing and maintaining trust is paramount. 33 The old adage of "always be closing" is being replaced by a new imperative: "always be discoverable, credible, and solution focused". 33 Transparency is a cornerstone of this trust - building exercise. Companies should openly communicate their sustainability goals, progress against these goals, and even the challenges they face in their journey, as this authenticity resonates with modern buyers. 47  Being candid about both the strengths and limitations of offerings, rather than over -promising, can also foster credibility. 33

Given the declining influence of traditional analyst reports 46 , leveraging authentic third - party validation has become even more critical. This includes actively cultivating and showcasing user reviews, encouraging peer endorsements, and developing genuine case studies that demonstrate tangible results. 33 Past positive experiences also play a significant role; research indicates that 84% of buyers ultimately purchase from a company they have worked with before. 33 This highlights the importance of nurturing existing customer relationships to foster loyalty and create advocates who can provide powerful third - party validation.

## Optimizing for Discoverability: SEO, Content Strategy, Influencer Engagement

In a buyer-led market, being easily discoverable at the precise moment a buyer is seeking solutions is crucial. Marketers should heed the advice to "Show Up Early: Don't wait for a demo request or a market qualified lead. Be everywhere". 33 This necessitates a robust online presence. With 72% of B2B buyers encountering Google's AI Overviews during their research and 90% of them clicking through to at least one source for verification 42 , a strong Search Engine Optimization (SEO) strategy and a concerted effort to be featured in trusted, authoritative source content are vital.

Content strategy should focus on developing value - driven narratives that clearly connect a vendor's offerings to the most pressing challenges and strategic aspirations of energy sector buyers. 49 This means moving beyond product features to articulate how solutions deliver specific outcomes and contribute to the buyer's success. Furthermore, collaboration with external influencers -such as respected industry journalists, influential podcasters, and niche subject matter experts -can significantly amplify reach and credibility. These influencers are increasingly shaping B2B buyer decisions, particularly among younger demographics. 49 In the APAC region, where buyers actively seek validation from their networks, managing and engaging with external influencers is a critical component of a successful GTM strategy. 34

## Value - Centric Selling: Addressing Specific Pains and Aspirations

The focus of sales and marketing efforts must shift from aggressively pushing products to genuinely helping buyers solve their problems and achieve their strategic objectives. 49 This requires a deep and empathetic understanding of customer needs, which can be cultivated through thorough persona research, journey mapping, and direct engagement. 41 B2B International's work in the utilities sector, for example, emphasizes the importance of understanding the diverse and often nuanced needs of business energy users. 53

Delivering value should not cease once a sale is made; fostering long - term relationships through ongoing support and value creation is key to retention and advocacy. 49  Forrester advises B2B marketing leaders to ensure their strategies are closely aligned with overall business goals, deeply rooted in customer understanding, flexible enough to adapt to changing market conditions, channel - agnostic in their reach, and designed to accommodate the complex dynamics of buying networks. 49 In the growing European B2B energy services market, for instance, a detailed, stage-bystage understanding of the customer's specific situation and requirements is essential for providers to offer tailored and effective solutions. 15

## Navigating Complex Buying Committees: Multi - Threaded Engagement

Marketers must recognize and adapt to the reality that B2B technology decisions in the energy sector typically involve large and diverse buying committees, often comprising 10 to 13 stakeholders from various functional areas. 33 Effectively engaging such a group requires a multi -threaded approach.

The first step is to diligently map out the full buying group, including not only internal decision-makers and users but also influential external parties such as consultants or partners. 34 Understanding the specific role, motivations, concerns, and influence level of each member is critical. Strategies must then be developed to build consensus among these diverse stakeholders, a process that Gartner identifies as a crucial final stage of th e buying journey. 50 This involves tailoring communication and value propositions to address the unique needs and priorities of each persona within the buying group, ensuring that the proposed solution resonates across different functional perspectives. 34

## Leveraging Data and Analytics for GTM Optimization

Data and analytics are indispensable tools for optimizing GTM strategies in the modern B2B environment. Investing in data capabilities allows marketers to gain a deeper understanding of customer needs, preferences, and behaviors, and to effectively decode  buying signals that indicate intent or progression through the journey. 49 The strategic use of intent data enables marketers to deliver the right message through the right channel at the right time, significantly increasing relevance and engagement. 41

Developing predictive, data -driven customer insights is crucial for proactive and targeted marketing. Accenture highlights that leading companies in digital sales effectively utilize big data analytics, combining customer feedback with transactional data t o create highly targeted offerings and identify opportunities for cross - selling and upselling. 54 Consistently tracking and measuring GTM performance using key metrics is also essential for assessing ROI, identifying areas for improvement, and making informed, data -driven business decisions. 45

## Recommendations for Regional Go - To- Market Strategies

While certain strategic principles apply globally, effective GTM execution in the energy technology market demands significant regional adaptation.

- ● General Approach: Across all regions, GTM strategies must align with the behaviors of the self -directed, digitally savvy buyer. The core focus should be on providing unambiguous value and building trust through credible, easily accessible content and verifiable third - party  validation. Marketers must also prepare for and strategically integrate the growing role of AI in how buyers conduct their research and form initial perceptions. 33 Bain &amp; Company advises that effective route - to- market strategies involve deploying resources efficiently to reach customers through the most appropriate channels, aligning sales efforts with the greatest opportunities, and making informed decisions about when to use in-person sales versus virtual or channel -based approaches. 55 While these principles are universal, their specific implementation must be tailored to regional nuances.
- ● North America: In this market, GTM strategies should emphasize digital channels and provide robust self -service options, particularly for products or services where buyers may already have a degree of familiarity. 43 Content and solutions should prominently feature capabilities relevant to regional priorities, such as grid modernization, renewable energy integration, powering the burgeoning data center industry, and addressing cybersecurity vulnerabilities. 9 The region's high spending on cybersecurity presents a clear opportunity for targeted messaging around security solutions.
- ● EMEA: GTM efforts in Europe should focus on the specific needs of the B2B energy services market, targeting key industrial segments with solutions for energy efficiency, on -site generation, EV infrastructure, hydrogen applications, and CCUS. 15  Building partnerships and adopting collaborative approaches are often crucial for delivering complex decarbonization solutions in this region. 32 Marketers should also note the lower encounter rates with AI - generated search overviews among EMEA buyers when planning their digital content strategies. 42
- ● APAC: Success in the APAC region hinges on extensive customization of GTM strategies at a country level, owing to the vast cultural, linguistic, and business practice diversity. 34 Messaging must be carefully localized, and sales approaches adapted to suit local norms. Strategies need to cater to the preferences of the predominantly younger, digital - first, and AI- savvy buyer demographic, which also places a high value on ESG and DEI  commitments. 40 Marketers should prepare for

engagement with larger buying committees and anticipate a higher volume of vendor interactions throughout the sales cycle. 33  Due to weaker brand loyalty in the region, continuous brand visibility and value demonstration are essential. 41 The high encounter rate with AI Overviews in search results also means that optimizing content for these platforms is particularly important in APAC. 42

The shift towards a buyer-led market, where individuals conduct extensive selfresearch before engaging vendors  33 , necessitates an "always-on" marketing imperative. Traditional, time-bound campaign-based thinking is becoming insufficient. Instead, marketing must function as a persistent, readily accessible resource hub, continuously providing value through high-quality content, interactive tools, and clear pathways to deeper information. This approach ensures that a vendor's brand is top-of-mind and their solutions are easily discoverable whenever a buyer actively enters a problem-solving or selection phase in their non-linear journey. 45 Forrester's advice to foster "consistent engagement with customers, turning each marketing initiative into a conversation rather than a one-off effort," and to create "adaptable programs that can pivot as needed," directly supports this "always-on" model. 49

Furthermore, regional GTM strategy is evolving beyond simple content localization to require a fundamental re-architecture based on the varying levels of buyer maturity, technological adoption, and deep-seated cultural contexts observed globally. The distinct characteristics of APAC buyers-their youth, digital nativity, larger committee sizes, weaker incumbent loyalty, high adoption of AI in research, and significant number of vendor interactions  33 -demand more than a superficial adaptation of Western GTM models. A successful strategy for APAC, for example, might necessitate different primary channel focuses (e.g., greater emphasis on local social media influencers or community engagement platforms), content formats optimized for mobile-first consumption and interactivity, and a sales process explicitly designed for longer engagement cycles and complex consensus-building within larger, culturally diverse committees. This deeper level of regional attunement is becoming a key differentiator for global B2B technology providers.

## Conclusion and Future Outlook

The B2B technology buying landscape within the global energy sector is undergoing a period of dynamic and transformative change. Driven by the urgent imperatives of the energy transition, decarbonization, evolving economic conditions, and heightened

ESG expectations, energy companies are fundamentally rethinking their operations and technology investments. This creates both significant opportunities and complex challenges for B2B technology providers.

Key technology investment priorities are coalescing around AI and GenAI for operational optimization and innovation, cloud computing for agility and data management, robust cybersecurity to protect increasingly connected infrastructure, and a suite of specialized solutions-including REMS, energy storage, grid modernization, hydrogen technologies, and CCUS-to enable the shift to a cleaner energy future. However, the very technologies enabling this transition, such as AI, are also creating new energy demand pressures, highlighting a complex interdependency.

The B2B buyer in the energy sector is more informed, self-directed, and skeptical than ever before. Large, multi-functional buying committees are the norm, and their decision-making processes are non-linear and often lengthy. Trust is a critical currency, earned through transparency, demonstrable value, and credible third-party validation, such as peer reviews and authentic case studies, rather than traditional marketing rhetoric or even, increasingly, mainstream analyst reports. Digital channels, particularly search (now heavily influenced by AI-generated overviews) and vendor websites, are primary research avenues, though strategic human interaction remains vital for complex decision-making and consensus-building.

Regional variations in buyer behavior, technology priorities, and GTM effectiveness are pronounced. North America is characterized by significant clean energy investments and grid modernization efforts. EMEA is seeing a boom in B2B energy services focused on industrial decarbonization and e-mobility. APAC presents a unique landscape with its younger, digital-native buyers, larger buying committees, emphasis on localized cultural nuances, and rapid adoption of AI in the research process.

For B2B technology marketers aiming to succeed in this evolving energy sector, several strategic pillars are essential:

- ● Embrace Value -Centricity and Trust - Building: Shift from product - centric selling to a consultative approach focused on solving buyer problems and delivering quantifiable value. Prioritize transparency and authenticity in all communications to build lasting trust.
- ●
- Master Digital Discoverability and Engagement: Invest in a robust "always -

on" content strategy that ensures visibility across key digital channels. Optimize for search, including AI-driven results, and leverage credible third-party platforms and influencers.

- ● Navigate Buying Committee Complexity: Develop deep understanding of the various personas within the energy tech buying committee, tailoring messaging and engagement to their specific roles, motivations, and pain points. Facilitate consensus-building.
- ● Adapt GTM Strategies Regionally: Move beyond superficial localization to develop genuinely tailored GTM approaches that respect and respond to distinct regional buying customs, technological maturity, and cultural contexts.
- ● Integrate AI Strategically but Responsibly: Leverage AI to enhance marketing efficiency and personalize buyer experiences, but also be mindful of buyer skepticism towards unverified AI -generated content. Ensure a foundation of human-validated, credible information.

Looking ahead, the pace of technological change and the pressures of the energy transition will only intensify. B2B technology providers that can successfully navigate this complexity by deeply understanding their energy sector customers, delivering innovative and reliable solutions with clear ESG benefits, and adapting their engagement strategies to the modern buyer will be best positioned for sustained growth and impact in this critical global industry. The ability to act as a trusted partner, helping ene rgy companies manage the dual challenge of meeting today's energy needs while building tomorrow's sustainable energy systems, will be the ultimate differentiator.
